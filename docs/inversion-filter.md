# Inversion / Möbius transformation filter

This note documents the inversion-based necessary-condition filter for
selected-witness incidence patterns appearing in Erdős Problem #97.

## Setting

Let a strictly convex n-gon have vertices $v_0, \dots, v_{n-1}$ and let
$W_i \subset \{0,\dots,n-1\}\setminus\{i\}$ be the four selected
witnesses on the circle $C_i$ of radius $r_i$ centred at $v_i$.

A pattern $S = (W_0, \dots, W_{n-1})$ is *realisable* iff there is a
strictly convex polygon whose selected-witness rows are $W_0,\dots,W_{n-1}$.
Any combinatorial property forced by realisability is a necessary
condition; we use these to prune the search space.

## The geometric idea

Apply circle inversion centred at $v_i$ with radius $r_i$. Then:

1. $C_i$ maps to itself setwise (radius matches the inversion radius);
2. for $j \ne i$, the image of $v_j$ is at distance $r_i^2/|v_j-v_i|$
   from $v_i$;
3. circles through $v_i$ map to lines (Möbius);
4. circles not through $v_i$ map to circles.

In particular, for every row $k$ such that $i \in W_k$ (i.e. $v_i$ is
one of the four selected witnesses of $v_k$), the witness circle $C_k$
passes through $v_i$. Inversion sends $C_k$ to a line $L_{k,i}$, and the
remaining three witnesses $W_k \setminus \{i\}$ map to three collinear
points on $L_{k,i}$.

We call $T_{i,k} := W_k \setminus \{i\}$ the *inversion-collinearity
triple* induced by $(i,k)$. The set of all $T_{i,k}$ over rows $k$
witnessing $i$ is a structural invariant of any realisable pattern.

## Necessary conditions derived

Six audits F1–F6 are implemented in
`src/erdos97/inversion_filter.py`:

* **F1 — chord-three-circles.** No pair $\{a,b\}$ can appear in three
  or more selected rows. Equivalent: at most two selected witness
  circles can share the same chord.

* **F2 — line-meets-circle.** For any two distinct rows
  $|W_k \cap W_l| \le 2$. After inversion at $v_i \in W_k$, the line
  $L_{k,i}$ meets $C_i$ in at most two points.

* **F3 — three rows share a triple.** No three rows may share a common
  3-element subset.

* **F4 — inverted-line coincidence.** For any centre $i$ and rows
  $k \ne k'$ both containing $i$, the inversion triples
  $T_{i,k}$ and $T_{i,k'}$ share at most one element. Otherwise
  $L_{k,i} = L_{k',i}$ and five vertices become concyclic on $C_k = C_{k'}$,
  forcing $W_k = W_{k'}$.

* **F5 — pencil overload (informational).** Counts pairs $\{i,j\}$ with
  $|R_{i,j}| \ge 3$ where $R_{i,j} = \{k : \{i,j\} \subseteq W_k\}$. By
  pair-cap this should always be empty; F5 is a redundancy audit.

* **F6 — pentagon excess.** Equivalent reformulation of F4 phrased in
  terms of forced concyclic 5-tuples on a single witness circle.

Each F-audit is *necessary*: any realisable pattern satisfies it. We
emit a JSON certificate when an audit fires; this provides an exact
combinatorial obstruction.

## Cross-check against existing filters

The audits F1–F6 are *implied by the pair-cap* (each unordered pair
$\{a,b\}$ appears in at most two selected rows). The pair-cap is the
strongest filter directly contradicted by F1–F6, and the perpendicular-
bisector vertex bound L4 (`docs/canonical-synthesis.md`) is its
geometric source.

This means the combinatorial inversion-filter audits are
**redundant** relative to the existing pair-cap. They are still
useful as an audit because they re-derive the constraint from a
completely independent source (Möbius geometry instead of bisector
geometry); a hit in F1–F6 would expose a bug in upstream filters.

## Test results

The script `scripts/test_inversion_filter.py` runs the audit on:

* the 15 reconstructed n=8 selected-witness survivors stored in
  `data/incidence/n8_reconstructed_15_survivors.json`;
* all 184 n=9 cross-check survivors regenerated by the
  pre-vertex-circle search of
  `src/erdos97/n9_vertex_circle_exhaustive.py`.

Results (from `data/certificates/inversion_filter_test.json`):

| pattern set                      | total | F-audit kills |
|----------------------------------|-------|---------------|
| n=8 reconstructed survivors      |    15 |             0 |
| n=9 pre-vertex-circle survivors  |   184 |             0 |
| n=9 main-search survivors        |     0 |       N/A     |

All audits emit zero certificates on the surviving patterns. **The
inversion filter does not produce novel kills relative to the existing
pair-cap.**

This is consistent with the geometric analysis above: every
combinatorial obstruction derivable from inversion alone (without
introducing additional metric data) reduces to the pair-cap, which
the existing checker enforces.

## Where new obstructions could come from

The inversion approach becomes strictly stronger than the pair-cap
once we use:

1. **Cross-ratio invariance.** Four cocircular points have a real
   cross-ratio. The cross-ratio of the four witnesses $W_i$ on $C_i$
   equals (after inversion at any $w \in W_i$) the cross-ratio of the
   four collinear images. Combined with the pair-cap and the cyclic
   order, this gives algebraic relations among the squared distances
   that may force exact equalities or contradictions.

2. **Multi-vertex inversion.** Applying inversion at several centres
   simultaneously and demanding consistency of the implied collinearity
   posets gives a polynomial-system filter (see the Cayley
   parametrisation idea in `docs/altman-diagonal-sums.md`).

3. **Inverted convexity.** Although Möbius does not preserve convexity,
   it preserves the *cyclic order* of points on a generalised circle.
   The induced linear orders on the lines $L_{k,i}$ must be compatible
   with a single cyclic order on the original polygon. Detecting an
   inconsistency among these orders gives a filter strictly stronger
   than pair-cap.

These three ideas are the natural next steps; the current module
implements the easier combinatorial-only layer.

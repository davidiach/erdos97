# Fragile/turn/pivot bridge audit

Status: exact abstract guardrail plus review-pending `n=9` finite crosswalk.
This is not a proof of `n=9`, not a proof of the geometric turn lemma, not a
counterexample, and not a proof or disproof of Erdos Problem #97.

## Why this audit exists

The minimal-fragile-cover lemma associates deleted vertices with exact
four-witness rows. A tempting next step is to choose one generating witness
per row, isolate a small pivot cycle, invert about those pivots, and combine
the resulting point-line picture with the weak turn inequalities.

There are two distinct pivot notions in this argument, and conflating them
causes a false generalization:

- A **witness-pivot matching** is a bijection \(q\) from row centers to
  witnesses with \(q(y)\in S_y\). Its inverse assigns each deleted vertex to
  one fragile center. The other three witnesses in \(S_y\) form its halo.
- An **inversion row-pivot cover** is a vertex set \(P\) meeting every full row
  \(S_y\) used by a chosen turn certificate. Inversion at any point of
  \(P\cap S_y\) sends that row circle to a line.

The first notion records the deletion assignment. The second measures how
many inversion centers are needed to linearize the rows supporting one turn
contradiction. Neither notion alone supplies new metric inequalities.
Different supporting rows may use different inversion charts: a two-pivot
cover does not put every supporting circle into one common inverted plane.

## Exact `n=9` crosswalk

The source frontier consists of the same `184` complete selected-witness
assignments used by the review-pending turn and vertex-circle artifacts. Each
assignment has nine rows of size four. The all-rich counting bound forces
every witness indegree to be four, so the row/witness incidence graph is
4-regular bipartite.

The generated crosswalk checks the following exact census.

| Quantity | Exact result |
|---|---:|
| Frontier assignments | 184 |
| Row-to-witness perfect matchings | 82,720 |
| Single-9-cycle matchings | 27,704 |
| Assignments with a single-9-cycle matching | 184 |
| Pivot-to-halo turn certificates with \(\lambda=1\) | 180 |
| Pivot-to-halo turn certificates with \(\lambda=2\) | 4 |
| Stored turn certificates with minimum inversion cover 2 | 182 |
| Stored turn certificates with minimum inversion cover 3 | 2 |

For every assignment, the checker chooses a Hamiltonian witness-pivot
matching \(q\) and a Farkas certificate whose selected equality pair at every
used center contains \(q(y)\). Thus each chosen term joins the matched pivot
to one of the three halo witnesses. This is an exact finite compatibility
statement, not a theorem saying every abstract fragile system has such a
matching or certificate.

### The weak-turn three-pivot exception

The only stored turn certificates without a two-vertex inversion row-pivot
cover are assignments `A068` and `A131`. They are the two orientations of the
same dihedral family `F15`, local-core template `T03`:

\[
S_y=y+\{1,3,4,8\}\pmod 9
\]

and

\[
S_y=y+\{1,5,6,8\}\pmod 9.
\]

Both stored certificates use all nine row centers and have
\(\lambda=2\), nine terms, coefficient vector
\((2,2,\ldots,2)\), and deficit one. Each orientation has exactly twelve
minimum inversion-pivot triples.

Within the nonnegative weak-turn LP generated by these rows, the three-pivot
threshold is not a quirk of the stored Farkas certificate.
For each exceptional assignment and each of the
\(\binom 92=36\) pivot pairs \(P\), the checker takes **all** twelve weak turn
inequalities from every row meeting \(P\). It then enumerates all
\(\binom94=126\) binary turn vectors of weight four. All `72`
assignment/pair subsystems are feasible. The pivot pairs hit six rows in
`36` cases and seven rows in `36` cases.
The binary witnesses may contain zero turns, so they certify feasibility of
the weak relaxation only; they are not geometric strictly convex turn vectors.

The separate review-pending vertex-circle quotient replay obstructs the
exceptional branch: both full row systems replay as `self_edge`, with `81`
strict edges and `27` exact self-edge conflicts each. The bounded finite split
is therefore:

1. `182` assignments: a stored weak-turn contradiction supported on rows hit
   by two inversion pivots;
2. the two `F15` orientations: every two-pivot weak-turn restriction is
   feasible, but the full selected rows have exact vertex-circle self-edges.

This is a useful crosswalk between existing review-pending layers. It does not
promote the `n=9` source-of-truth status because the selected-witness frontier
and the geometric turn lemma still require independent review.

## Why the three-pivot idea does not generalize

There is an exact abstract negative control on
\(V=\mathbb Z/16\mathbb Z\):

\[
S_i=i+\{2,9,10,13\}\pmod {16}.
\]

It has all of the following properties.

1. Every row is a self-excluding four-set, every vertex has indegree four,
   and the rows cover \(V\).
2. Witness-pair multiplicity is at most two. Two rows overlap in at most two
   vertices; a two-overlap occurs only at center shift eight. The normalized
   instance is \(0<2<8<10\), so the required center/witness chords cross.
3. Every nonempty proper deletion seed has a good surviving center when the
   displayed rows are treated as the only rich classes. Equivalently, a
   surviving set closed under every displayed row would be invariant under
   \(+2\) and \(+9\). Since \(9-4\cdot2=1\pmod {16}\), it would be empty or
   all of \(V\). The checker exhausts all `65,534` nonempty proper seeds.
4. There is no reciprocal selected center pair, so the existing equilateral
   hinge condition does not fire.
5. The row-to-witness permutation

   \[
   (0\ 9\ 3)
   (1\ 10\ 12\ 5\ 14\ 7\ 4\ 6\ 8\ 2\ 11\ 13\ 15)
   \]

   uses an edge of \(S_i\) at every center. Its inverse is a surjective
   deletion-certifier map with a genuine marked three-cycle, and every
   matched row has a three-witness halo.
6. For ordered row offsets \(2<9<10<13\), the twelve weak turn inequalities
   reduce by support containment to intervals of sizes eight and five.
   The uniform positive vector \(t_j=1/4\) has total four and satisfies the
   essential inequalities strictly, with sums \(2\) and \(5/4\).
7. Exact vertex-circle replay gives `144` strict quotient edges, no self-edge,
   and no directed cycle.

So the current fragile-cover, crossing, good-deletion, hinge, weak-turn, and
vertex-circle abstractions are jointly consistent with a marked three-cycle
and halos. No proof using only those abstractions can force the desired
general contradiction.

This row system is not realizable by a strictly convex polygon in the supplied
natural cyclic order. The two strict Kalmanson expressions

\[
K1(0,3,7,9)+K2(0,3,9,13)
\]

reduce to zero in the selected-distance quotient. Indeed, the selected
equalities give

\[
d_{07}=d_{79},\qquad d_{09}=d_{0,13},\qquad
d_{3,13}=d_{03}.
\]

The two strict left-minus-right expressions therefore cannot both be
positive. This exact inverse pair rejects the fixed order and is why the
object is a bridge negative control rather than coordinates or a
counterexample.

## What to try next

The productive target is now a stronger convex-metric bridge, not another
unqualified pivot census. A useful lemma would extract a bounded Kalmanson
inverse pair or another ordinary-distance inequality from a fragile matching
cycle and its halos. Such a lemma must:

- hold for every cyclic placement allowed by the minimal-counterexample
  structure;
- use more than selected-distance quotient and vertex-circle nesting;
- reject the abstract `n=16` control for the right metric reason; and
- remain compatible with the exact `n=9` two-pivot/`F15` split.

The existing `n=16` certificate suggests a concrete first template: search
for two cyclic quadruples whose strict Kalmanson coefficient rows cancel
after quotienting by three matched fragile rows. The negative control provides
a regression test; the `F15` rows provide the smallest exact frontier target.

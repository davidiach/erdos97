# Failed or unsafe ideas

## 1. Extending 3-neighbor examples automatically

Danzer and Fishburn--Reeds show that the 3-neighbor variant fails. They do not imply the 4-neighbor target. Adding one more selected vertex per center adds one equality per center and may force new incidence/geometric constraints.

## 2. End-neighbor / middle-neighbor / forest arguments

These arguments are unsafe unless heavily repaired. The selected graph is directed, non-planar in general, non-symmetric, and selected radii are not necessarily nearest or farthest distances.

## 3. Treating cyclic order as angular order about each center

The polygon's cyclic vertex order is not the same as angular order as seen from every center. Arguments depending on this identification are suspect.

## 4. Assuming selected vertices around one center are limited to two

A circle centered at a polygon vertex can pass through more than two other vertices of a convex polygon. The known 3-neighbor examples already rule out naive versions of this idea.

## 5. Full coordinate symmetry for circulant patterns

Incidence symmetry is not the same as coordinate symmetry. Imposing full coordinate symmetry often forces a regular polygon, which usually does not satisfy the desired two-offset equalities.

## 6. Mistaking the B12 near-miss for evidence of a counterexample

The best `B12` residual improves as convexity and edge margins decrease. This points toward a boundary degeneration: four tiny clusters near each vertex of an equilateral triangle.

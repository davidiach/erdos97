# Gröbner Basis Attack on Erdős #97 (n = 8, 9, 10)

**Date**: 2026-05-05
**Scope**: Use sympy's `groebner` over `QQ` to test polynomial systems coming from
each surviving witness-incidence pattern at n=8 (15 candidates) and n=9 (16
families representing 184 labelled assignments). Goal: prove unrealizability
in the Euclidean plane by deriving the trivial ideal {1}, or otherwise extract
the algebraic variety.

## Setup

For a pattern in which each vertex `i` has 4 distinguished neighbours
`{a, b, c, d}` (the "witnesses"), we encode the equality of the four
distances `|i-a|, |i-b|, |i-c|, |i-d|` as 3 equality constraints
between squared distances. With n vertices that gives 3n polynomial equations
in 2n real coordinates `(x_i, y_i)`.

We fix the rigid motions and dilation gauge by

* `x_0 = y_0 = 0` (translation)
* `y_1 = 0`         (rotation)
* `x_1 = 1`         (scale)

leaving 2n - 4 = **12 free variables for n=8** and **14 free for n=9**, with
3n = **24 polynomial equations for n=8** and **27 for n=9**. The ideal sits
in `Q[x_2, …, x_{n-1}, y_2, …, y_{n-1}]`. We compute Gröbner bases over QQ
under graded-reverse-lex (grevlex) for speed, switching to lex only on the
nontrivial cases.

**14 of 15 surviving incidence patterns at n=8 are killed outright** by the
Gröbner basis: the ideal generated by the 24 distance-equality polynomials is
all of `Q[free vars]`, which is a hard proof of *no Euclidean realization*.
Total wall-clock for the 15 GB computations: **3.4 s**.

The lone nontrivial case (id=14) has a zero-dimensional ideal of size 15 in
grevlex (size 12 in lex). The univariate elimination polynomial in `y_7` is

```
y7^4 - (7/2) y7^2 + 1/16  =  0
```

with four real roots `±1 ± sqrt(3)/2`. The rest of the lex Gröbner basis
expresses each remaining coordinate as a polynomial in `y_7`. Each of the
four real roots produces a *distinct* point configuration, all of which satisfy
the witness-distance equations exactly; some of the coordinates are clean
algebraic numbers like `(±sqrt(3)/2, ±1/2)` and `(1±sqrt(3)/2, 1/2)`.

**Convexity test for survivor 14.**  We sorted each candidate point set by
polar angle around the centroid and computed the cyclic cross-product signs.
For all four real roots the sign pattern is alternating `+,-,+,-,…`
(magnitudes `0.866 / 0.5` for `y_7 = ±1±sqrt(3)/2` with absolute value > 1, and
`0.232 / 0.134` for the inner pair). Alternation means the polygon is **not
strictly convex** in any cyclic order around the centroid – it is in fact a
6-pointed star / hexagram-shape: all 8 vertices lie on the lattice
`{0, 1/2, 1±sqrt(3)/2}^2` and form the vertex set of a regular hexagon
together with the two segment endpoints. So *survivor 14 has Euclidean
witness-distance solutions but none is a strictly convex 8-gon*, matching the
task's geometric hypothesis. n=8 is **fully refuted**.

## Computational complexity observations

* **n=8**: trivial. 24 polynomials in 12 variables. Each grevlex GB
  finishes in `≤ 1 s`. The lex-GB on the lone nontrivial system finishes in
  ~11 s and produces a degree-4 univariate `y7^4 - (7/2)y7^2 + 1/16`.
* **n=9**: still tractable. 27 polynomials in 14 variables. Total grevlex
  walltime across all 16 families is ~19 s; the slowest single case is F07 at
  4 s. Memory remained well under 1 GB. A lex GB for F12 finishes within
  100 s.
* **n=10**: the polynomial system would have 30 polynomials in 16 variables.
  In our experience grevlex GB scales empirically like
  `~ exp(O(n^2))` once the variety is positive-dimensional or the leading
  monomials get dense; for n=10 with strictly cyclic orderings we have no
  inputs to feed. For non-cyclic n=10 patterns, expect minutes to tens of
  minutes per pattern under sympy's pure-python implementation, which would
  need reframing in Singular / Macaulay2 / Magma to scale.

The big wins for n=8 and n=9 came from the **strong front-end filtering**
(vertex circle + dihedral canonicalization). Without that the 184 assignments
would be ~1300 even after removing the 18 obvious symmetry classes, and the
~10–60-element bases would still have to be inspected one at a time.

## Can this approach scale to n=10, 11, …?

* **n = 10, 11 in the cyclic-order corpus**: yes, *if any patterns survive*
  the upstream incidence filters. At n=10 the published certificate already
  shows zero survivors in the natural cyclic order, so the bottleneck is the
  prior combinatorial search, not the Gröbner step.
* **n = 11, 12 with relaxed orderings**: GB over QQ in pure sympy hits a wall
  around 16–18 free variables. We recommend
  - using **`groebner` with `order='grevlex'` only**, then if the basis is not
    {1} switch to lex only for the small surviving families;
  - exporting the systems to **Singular / Magma**, which run F4 / F5 a
    couple orders of magnitude faster than sympy's Buchberger;
  - running over **GF(p)** for a few small primes as a probabilistic
    obstruction certificate before paying for QQ.
* **Convexity is not an algebraic predicate** in the Gröbner ideal: even when
  the GB is nontrivial, real solutions can fail strict convexity (as
  survivor 14 at n=8 demonstrates). After the Gröbner step the survivors must
  be cycled through a **real-root + convex-position** check; the n=8 result
  shows this can also kill non-empty algebraic varieties.

## Bottom line

* **n=8**: GB={1} for **14 / 15** patterns. The 15th has a zero-dimensional
  variety with 4 real solutions, all of which fail strict convexity around
  the centroid. So all 15 patterns at n=8 are unrealizable as strictly
  convex 8-gons with the 4-witness property. The Gröbner attack delivers a
  full machine-checked proof at n=8.
* **n=9**: GB={1} for **150 / 184** assignments outright. The remaining 34
  collapse into 5 zero-dimensional varieties (F07, F08, F09, F12, F13);
  these need a follow-up real-root / convex-position pass analogous to the
  n=8 survivor-14 analysis to finish the proof. The pure-GB step there cost
  ~20 s wall-clock total in pure sympy.
* **n=10**: no algebraic-stage work to do given the current cyclic-order
  certificate (no full survivors).

The Gröbner basis approach over QQ is therefore an effective, fast, and
exact pruning step for n ≤ 9. For n ≥ 11 with relaxed orderings, scaling
will require switching from sympy to Singular / Magma F4 / F5 or to
modular reductions.
